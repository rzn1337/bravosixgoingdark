A good note is short enough to write quickly and clear enough to read later.
When I keep the wording plain, I spend less time second guessing myself. The
point is to capture the thought, not to polish it. Later I can expand the parts
that turn out to matter and delete the rest.

Planning works best when it stays flexible. I sketch a rough order, start with
the first step, and adjust as I learn more. A plan that is too detailed tends to
break on contact with reality, while a plan that is too vague gives no
direction at all. Somewhere in the middle is a list I can actually follow.

Focus is a limited resource, so I try to spend it on the hard problems early in
the day. Routine tasks can wait for the quieter hours when my attention has
drifted. Grouping similar work together saves the small cost of switching, and
that cost adds up more than people expect.

Review is the step most often skipped. Ten minutes at the end of a session to
note what happened and what to do next makes the following start far easier.
The habit is simple, but the payoff compounds. Over a week those short reviews
turn a pile of loose activity into steady progress.
